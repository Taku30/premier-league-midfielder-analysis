Identifying Midfielder Archetypes in the Premier League Using Football Performance Data

Author

Taku Takahashi

Introduction

Modern football has evolved significantly over the past decade, particularly in midfield roles. Midfielders are no longer evaluated solely based on technical quality such as passing range or creativity. In today’s game, midfielders are increasingly expected to contribute across multiple phases of play, including defensive transitions, pressing intensity, positional awareness, and physical output.

The modern game has become faster, more transition-oriented, and more physically demanding. As a result, elite midfielders are often required to combine technical ability with defensive contribution, tactical discipline, and athletic intensity. Players who can contribute both offensively and defensively have become increasingly valuable in modern football systems.

Because of these tactical developments, football clubs and analysts increasingly rely on performance data and statistical profiling to support scouting and recruitment decisions.

This project explores how football statistics can be used to analyze and profile modern midfielders in the Premier League. The analysis focuses on identifying different midfielder characteristics and stylistic profiles using player performance data derived from FBref statistics.

Rather than focusing only on traditional attacking statistics, this project emphasizes broader midfielder responsibilities such as:

  • Defensive contribution
  • Creative involvement
  • Possession retention and involvement
  • Transitional contribution
  • Overall midfield balance

The project combines football understanding with data analysis techniques in order to better understand what statistically defines modern Premier League midfielders.

Project Objectives

The main objectives of this project are to:

  • Analyze statistical characteristics of Premier League midfielders
  • Explore how modern midfielder roles differ across playing styles
  • Identify different midfielder archetypes using performance metrics
  • Compare players based on stylistic similarities
  • Investigate undervalued or less recognized midfielder profiles

The project also aims to demonstrate how football analytics can support scouting and player evaluation in modern football environments.

Scope of Analysis

Although the original dataset contains player statistics from Europe’s top five leagues, this project focuses specifically on Premier League midfielders.

The Premier League was selected because of:

  • Its high physical intensity
  • Tactical competitiveness
  • Transition-heavy style of play
  • Strong global relevance in modern football analysis

Restricting the analysis to a single league also helps maintain a more consistent tactical and statistical environment when comparing midfielder profiles.

Data Source

The dataset used in this project contains player performance statistics from the completed 2025–2026 football season and is derived from FBref data.

FBref is one of the most widely recognized football statistics platforms used in football analytics and scouting communities. The platform provides detailed player and team statistics sourced from Opta, including:

  • Goal contribution metrics
  • Shooting statistics
  • Defensive actions
  • Match participation metrics
  • Discipline statistics
  • General player performance indicators

The dataset contains over 100 statistical variables related to player performance across multiple competitions and leagues.

For this project, the dataset is filtered specifically for Premier League midfielders in order to focus the analysis on modern midfield roles within a more consistent football environment.

Important Considerations

Football performance cannot be fully explained through statistics alone. Many important aspects of football remain difficult to quantify, including:

  • Tactical intelligence
  • Communication
  • Leadership
  • Off-ball movement
  • Team tactical structure
  • Decision-making under pressure

Therefore, this project should be viewed as a data-assisted football profiling and scouting exercise rather than a complete measure of player quality.

Additionally, player statistics can be influenced by:

  • Team tactics
  • Coaching systems
  • Teammate quality
  • Match context
  • Playing time and role consistency

For this reason, football context remains essential when interpreting statistical output.

Midfielder Archetype Analysis

Modern midfielders perform a wide variety of tactical and statistical roles within Premier League football.

Some midfielders contribute heavily in defensive phases, while others focus more on attacking output, ball progression, or balanced two-way play.

To better understand these stylistic differences, clustering analysis is used to group midfielders with similar statistical profiles into broader archetypes.

Per-90 Normalization

Raw cumulative statistics are heavily influenced by playing time and match involvement.

To better compare midfielder playing styles independent of total minutes played, several statistical variables are converted into per-90 metrics.

Per-90 normalization helps estimate contribution rates rather than total accumulation, allowing for more balanced player comparisons during clustering and similarity analysis.

To reduce statistical noise from extremely small sample sizes, players with fewer than 10 full 90-minute equivalents (900 minutes played) were excluded from the clustering and similarity analysis.

This filtering step improves the reliability of per-90 performance metrics and reduces distortion caused by limited playing time.

While per-90 normalization helps standardize player comparisons, minimum playing-time thresholds remain important in football analytics to ensure statistical stability and more meaningful interpretation.

midfielder_profiles <- prem_midfielders %>%
  select(
    player,
    squad,
    age,
    pos,
    x90s,
    gls,
    ast,
    g_a,
    sh,
    so_t,
    tkl_w,
    int,
    fls,
    crd_y,
    crd_r
  ) %>%
  filter(x90s >= 10) %>%
  mutate(
    gls_per90 = gls / x90s,
    ast_per90 = ast / x90s,
    g_a_per90 = g_a / x90s,
    sh_per90 = sh / x90s,
    sot_per90 = so_t / x90s,
    tkl_per90 = tkl_w / x90s,
    int_per90 = int / x90s,
    fls_per90 = fls / x90s
  )

Determining the Number of Clusters

Before performing clustering analysis, the Elbow Method is used to estimate an appropriate number of midfielder clusters.

The Elbow Method helps identify the point where adding additional clusters produces diminishing improvements in cluster separation.

midfielder_profiles <- midfielder_profiles %>%

  mutate(

    gls_per90 = pmin(gls_per90, quantile(gls_per90, 0.99)),

    ast_per90 = pmin(ast_per90, quantile(ast_per90, 0.99)),

    g_a_per90 = pmin(g_a_per90, quantile(g_a_per90, 0.99)),

    sh_per90 = pmin(sh_per90, quantile(sh_per90, 0.99)),

    sot_per90 = pmin(sot_per90, quantile(sot_per90, 0.99)),

    tkl_per90 = pmin(tkl_per90, quantile(tkl_per90, 0.99)),

    int_per90 = pmin(int_per90, quantile(int_per90, 0.99)),

    fls_per90 = pmin(fls_per90, quantile(fls_per90, 0.99))

  )

find_similar_players <- function(player_name, top_n = 5) {

  similarities <- sort(distance_matrix[player_name, ])

  similarities <- similarities[2:(top_n + 1)]

  tibble(
    similar_player = names(similarities),
    distance = similarities
  )
}

Conclusion

This project explored how statistical analysis and unsupervised learning techniques can be used to profile modern Premier League midfielders using football performance data derived from FBref.

Through exploratory analysis, per-90 normalization, clustering, and similarity modeling, the project identified several broad midfielder archetypes associated with different tactical and stylistic responsibilities.

The analysis demonstrated how:

  • Per-90 normalization

  • Playing-time thresholds

  • Clustering methods

  • Statistical similarity models

  • Football-specific interpretation

can support player profiling and football scouting workflows.

The project also highlighted the complexity of modern midfield roles. Many players demonstrated overlapping statistical profiles, reflecting the increasing tactical demands placed on midfielders in transition-heavy modern football.

Importantly, the results showed that statistical clustering captures behavioral performance patterns rather than traditional positional labels alone.

While football analytics can provide valuable insights, football performance cannot be fully explained through statistics alone. Tactical context, coaching systems, player responsibilities, and team structure remain essential when interpreting analytical output.

Overall, this project demonstrates how data analysis techniques can support more structured and evidence-based football evaluation while still requiring football-specific contextual understanding.

Limitations and Future Improvements

Although the dataset provides a broad range of football statistics, several limitations remain within the analysis.

The dataset does not include:

  • Detailed event-level data

  • Progressive carrying metrics

  • Pressure-related statistics

  • Spatial tracking data

  • Tactical positioning information
